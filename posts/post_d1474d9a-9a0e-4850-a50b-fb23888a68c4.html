Make and build stuff you need!

June 28, 2026 • Blog

The concept of reading at velocity is far more profound than simply moving your eyes across a page faster; it is fundamentally a lesson in mental efficiency and how we choose to engage with the world of information. As explored in my reflection on The Art of Velocity, I realized that true rapid reading is not about sacrificing comprehension for speed, but rather transforming the way the brain processes text. It requires us to shift our mindset entirely—moving away from the tedious task of decoding every single word to the more vital goal of instantly grasping the central meaning and context.

At the root of this transformation lies overcoming what I call subvocalization, that persistent habit of mentally speaking each word as I read it. When we read slowly, our minds get caught in this auditory loop, causing us to be passive consumers rather than active participants. To unlock genuine speed, the first step is to consciously relax this internal voice and train the eyes to engage visually with larger blocks of text. This allows the brain to leapfrog over unnecessary details and begin recognizing patterns, context, and overall structure immediately.

To manage this flow effectively, I discovered the power of strategic engagement techniques. Instead of forcing myself to read every sentence sequentially, I learned to employ skimming as my initial strategy. Before diving deep into a complex report or an article, I practice rapidly surveying headings, introductory paragraphs, and concluding summaries. This pre-reading process acts like building a roadmap for the information, priming the brain to anticipate what is coming next and allowing me to quickly assess the material’s main ideas before committing to detailed reading. Complementing this is scanning, which trains my eyes to instantly hunt for specific data points, names, or keywords. This skill allows me to bypass irrelevant information and jump directly to the core evidence I need.

Sustaining this accelerated pace demands strict control over my environment. Distractions are the most potent enemies of focus; external noise fragments the attention needed for deep processing. Therefore, cultivating speed requires creating a dedicated, distraction-free space where intense concentration can flourish. Internally, managing mental fatigue is just as crucial. When faced with dense or challenging material, instead of getting bogged down by minor details, I learned to practice mindful pausing—not to slow down, but to briefly stop and ask clarifying questions internally, immediately seeking the core message rather than chasing every peripheral thought.

Ultimately, mastering this art requires consistent, patient practice. Initially, attempting to read quickly can feel awkward or even frustrating as the brain adjusts to this new pattern. However, with repeated exposure to these techniques—skimming, scanning, and focused engagement—the mental pathways gradually develop this necessary efficiency. The goal is not just increased reading speed, but a deeper capacity for intellectual agility, allowing me to navigate the overwhelming volume of information with greater confidence and a true sense of understanding. It is about becoming a powerful processor of knowledge rather than just a passive recipient of text.
